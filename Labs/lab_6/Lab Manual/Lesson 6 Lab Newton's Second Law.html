Lesson 6 Lab: Newton's Second Law

Introduction

Sir Isaac Newton's second law of motion states that the acceleration of an object is proportional to the applied force and inversely proportional to its mass. These two claims can be combined into a single mathematical expression:

Fnet∝ma{"version":"1.1","math":"F_{net} \propto ma"}.

The proportionality can be replaced by an equal sign with the addition of a proportionality constant:

Fnet=kma{"version":"1.1","math":"F_{net} = kma"}.

If mass is measured in kg, acceleration in m/s2, and force in Newtons (1 N = 1 kg-m/s2) then the proportionality constant k = 1 and

Fnet=ma{"version":"1.1","math":"F_{net} = ma"}.

Since the equation has three variables, it is necessary to hold one constant while investigating the relationship between the other two. In the first part of this experiment, the total accelerating mass is held constant and the relationship between acceleration and applied force is explored. In the second part, the same force is applied to systems of differing mass and the acceleration of each system is measured.

Data Collection

Constant Mass

A Vernier motion detector was used to measure the position and velocity of a Pasco car moving on a horizontal track. One end of a string was attached to the front of the cart and the other end was attached to a 50-gram weight hanger suspended from a pulley.

The mass of the car was 250 grams. In addition to the car itself, a 250-g mass and three slotted weights ‐ two 20-g masses and a 10-g mass ‐ were also placed on the car. Therefore, a total of 600 grams was accelerated while the weight was falling.

When the weight hanger was released, the constant gravitational force of the falling mass was transferred through the string as a constant tension pulling the car forward. Since the gravitational force is the product of mass and the gravitational constant, the applied force was 0.49 N. A motion detector was positioned behind the car and it began collecting data of velocity as a function of time a few moments before the weight hanger was released.

After the first run, the 10-g mass was transferred from the car to the weight hanger, resulting in 60 grams of hanging mass and a 0.59 N applied force. Because the 10-g mass was still being accelerated, whether on the car or on the weight hanger, the total accelerating mass was still 600 grams. This process was repeated in 10-gram increments by moving the 20-g and 10-g masses back and forth between the car and the weight hanger.

Constant Force

For the second part of the investigation, the 50-gram weight hanger pulled on the 250-gram car for the first trial. In the second trial, a 250-gram mass was added to the car but the constant applied force was still just the weight hanger (0.49 N). Two more 250-gram masses were added, one at a time, for trials three and four.

Conclusions
  1. The equation for Newton's second law, F = ma, has a y-intercept of zero. How does the graph of force versus acceleration for a system of constant mass support this relationship?
  2. Compare the slope of the line to the total accelerating mass. Comment on the two values and the expected relationship between them if the second law is true.
  3. The equation, F = ma can be re-written as: a = F(1/m). How does the graph of acceleration versus inverse mass for a system of constant force support this relationship?
  4. Compare the slope of the line to the constant applied force. Comment on the two values and the expected relationship between them if the second law is true.
