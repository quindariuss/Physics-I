Lesson 10 Lab: Conservation of Momentum

Introduction

The principle of momentum conservation, like energy conservation, is a direct consequence of Newton's laws of motion. Unlike energy, momentum is a vector so the direction must be taken into account.

Conservation of momentum may be stated as the mathematical expression:

Σpinitial+J=Σpfinal{"version":"1.1","math":" \Sigma _{p_{initial} }+ J = \Sigma _{p_{final} } "}

where p is the symbol for momentum, p = mv, and J is the symbol for impulse, J = FΔt. In words, the expression may be read as:

The sum of the vector momenta of the particles in a system at some point in space and time, plus any impulses caused by external forces, must equal the sum of their momenta at a later time.

Conservation of momentum is the preferred method of solution for physical situations involving collisions and explosions. In this experiment, we will investigate ten different interactions between a blue and a red car. Differences between trial conditions will include: the elasticity of the interaction (perfectly elastic or perfectly inelastic); the mass of the cars (equal mass or one car twice the mass of the other); and the initial velocity of the cars (the cars either moving or at rest).

Data Analysis

Download all Logger ProTM *.cmbl files and movies into a common directory. It is necessary for the movie to be in the same directory as the Vernier file to load it into the program.

Start the Logger ProTM software and open the file Trial 1.cmbl. A data table will be to the left, the movie will be on the top, and a graph that has already been prepared for data collection will be below it. The title of the graph will state the conditions of the trial. The green line in the video was added to set the scale of the frames: it has a length of 1.2 meters.

Click on the 'play' button in the lower left-hand corner (it is shaped like a triangle pointing to the right) and play the movie. (Hovering over each button will show its purpose.) Click the third button in that row to reset the movie, or used the slider to pull back to the first frame.

Click the Point Series button, which is the sixth and last button in the vertical column to the right, to make sure you are analyzing the blue car first. Click the Set Point option, the second vertical button. When you move into the frame, you will see cross hairs. Center the cross hairs on one of the two wheels of the blue car. When you click on the wheel, the movie will advance to the next frame and your choice will be marked on the plot. Continue doing this for 5 - 15 frames or until you have enough data points to find the slope of the straight line on the graph.

Click on the Point Series button to choose the red car series and reset the movie. Repeat these actions for a wheel of the red car for a number of frames until you are confident you can correctly determine the constant speed of the red car.

Highlight a region of the graph where both the blue and red points seem to be showing a straight line by dragging over it. Click the Linear Fit button from the icons at the top of the program and check the boxes for both the blue and red cars. Report the slopes of the lines, including the positive or negative sign, as the final velocities of the cars to the closest hundredth of a meter per second (0.01 m/s) in the data table for the first trial.

Use the car masses and velocities to calculate initial and final momenta, and compare the sum of the initial and final momenta of the cars. Report the calculation of momenta to the closest hundredth of a unit as well.

Repeat this process for the remaining trials. It is not necessary to begin marking points form the first frame of a movie.

Determine the percent difference as the absolute value of difference between the initial and final momenta of the cars divided by the average of the initial and final momentum magnitudes, times 100 to make it a percent.

If the cars are initially at rest, divide by the average of the final momentum magnitudes of the two cars.

Example 1: the initial momenta are 0.31 and -0.42 kg-m/s; the final momenta are -0.35 and 0.22 kg-m/s. The percent difference is:

% difference = |(-0.11)-(-0.13)|/|(-0.11-0.13)/2|x100 = (0.02/0.12)x100 = 17%

If the cars are initially at rest, divide the difference of the final momentum magnitudes by the average final momentum magnitude.

Example 2: the two cars are at rest initially; after the explosion the momenta are 0.25 kg-m/s and -0.20 kg-m/s. The percent difference is

%difference = [|0.25-0.20|/(0.25+0.20)/2]x100 = 22%

Conclusions
  1. Given the individual calculations on percent difference, what would appear to be a reasonable percent difference for this experiment?
  2. In data analysis, there is a condition known as "the problem of small denominators". You may have noticed that the percent error was noticeably larger when the initial and final momenta were small in magnitude. Why was that?
  3. You should have noticed that for Trial 3, a perfectly elastic collision between two cars of equal mass, the first car came to a complete stop. Given that perfectly elastic collisions conserve both momentum and kinetic energy, why is this fact both reasonable and necessary?
  4. How does this experiment demonstrate that the vector direction of momentum, represented here as a plus or minus sign, is very important when considering momentum conservation? For example, by how much might the percent difference change if you forgot to make a velocity of a car and therefore the momentum negative when it was supposed to be in one of your trials?
