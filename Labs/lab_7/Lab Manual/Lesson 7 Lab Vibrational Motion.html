Lesson 7 Lab: Vibrational Motion

Introduction

If an object is following Hooke's Law, then

Fnet=−kx=ma{"version":"1.1","math":"F_{net} = - kx = ma"}

Since acceleration is the second derivative of position with respect to time, the relationship can be written as the differential equation:

−kx=m∂2x∂t2{"version":"1.1","math":"- kx = m\frac{∂^{2}x }{∂t^{2} } "}

Methods for solving differential equations are beyond the scope of this course; in fact, a course in differential equations is usually a requirement for a degree in engineering or physics. However, the solution to this differential equation is well known:

x(t)=Acos(ωt+δ){"version":"1.1","math":"x(t) = A cos(\omega t + \delta )"}

where A is the amplitude of vibration, δ is the phase shift angle and ω is the angular frequency measured in rad/sec. The amplitude and phase shift angle are arbitrary constants but the angular frequency is determined by the conditions of the system. For a mass vibrating horizontally or vertically on a spring, the relationship is:

ω=km{"version":"1.1","math":"\omega = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m} } "}

where k is the spring constant and m is the mass of the vibrating object.

The purpose of this investigation is to verify these and other relationships for a vibrating mass. At many institutions, it is possible to measure position, velocity and acceleration as a function time using appropriate hardware connected to a computer running Logger ProTM. Since that is not possible for an at-home experiment, real data has been collected and saved as a movie that is ready to be analyzed.

Basic Parameters

Use the Examine button to identify the maximum amplitude of vibration from the position vs. time plot and record it on the Report Sheet. Find the period of vibration by determining the time it takes the mass to complete one vibration. Do this by measuring the time it takes to complete a number of vibrations and dividing by that number, i.e.:

Calculate the counting frequency, f, as the inverse of the period: f = 1/T. Calculate the natural frequency, ω, as the counting frequency multiplied by 2π: ω = 2πf.

For this data set, the maximum amplitude is 0.013 m. The period T = (tf - ti)/n = (3.79-0.21)/8 = 0.45 sec. The frequency f = 1/T = 1/0.45 = 2.24 Hz, and the natural frequency ω = 2πf = 14.1 rad/s.

Force Constant and Natural Frequency

Open the Graph Options for the force vs. time plot. Un-check the Connect Points option. Plot position on the x-axis and force on the y-axis. The graph will demonstrate that the system is following Hooke's Law. Use the linear fit option to find the force constant from the slope of the data. Record the value on the Report Sheet.

Calculate the natural frequency from the constant and the mass of the hanging weight, then express the result in terms of counting frequency and period. Compare the theoretical results to the experimental values found in the previous section.

The mass for this vibrating system was also 100 grams. From the slope of the plot, k = 19.7 N/m. Therefore, ω = √(k/m) = √(19.7/0.100) = 14.0 rad/sec. The frequency f = ω/2π = 14.0/6.28 = 2.2 Hz, and the period T = 1/f = 1/2.2 = 0.45 sec.
