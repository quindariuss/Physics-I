1: Measurement

Lesson 1 Lab: Graphing Data

Introduction

 Most investigations in physics are concerned with how one quantity varies with respect to another. Examples from other experiments in this course include how position varies with time, how force varies with the length of a stretched rubber band, or how frictional force varies with normal force. Usually, the best way to understand these relationships is to examine the data graphically.

The Logger ProTM software is an excellent program for plotting and analyzing data. The purpose of this activity is to become familiar with the features of Logger ProTM by plotting the relationship between the period and length of the simple pendulum.

A simple pendulum consists of a small mass swinging from the end of a string. The period is the length of time it takes the object to swing back and forth once. Other investigations show that the mass of the swinging object doesn't affect the period; nor does its release point if the angle of displacement is not too large. For accurate results, the length of the pendulum should be measured from the point of rotation to the center of the bob, which is its center of mass.

Materials
  • Graphing Data Report Sheet (download and print)
  • A meter-long string
  • A small weight, like a key
  • A timer
  • Logger ProTM software

Instructions

These instructions include visual examples of data collection and completing each step of the experiment. Note that the sample data is not for the pendulum lengths specified in the data table on the Report Sheet. Also, it may be helpful to view the video clips of Logger ProTM in full screen mode for greater clarity.

Lay the weight and string on a flat surface and use a meter stick to measure a 20-cm distance from the center of the weight to a point on the string, then pinch the string at that point. Hold the string in the air with the mass hanging from it, pull the mass slightly off center, then measure the time it takes for the mass to swing back and forth ten times.

Record the time in the data table on the Report Sheet. Divide this number by ten to determine the period of the pendulum and record it in the data table as well. Repeat this process for the other pendulum lengths. 

Step 1: Working With The Data Table

Open Logger ProTM. Enter the length of the pendulum in the x-column and period in the y-column.

Double-click on the letter X underneath the words "Data Set" in the left-hand side of the screen.

  • Under Name: replace 'X' with 'Length'.
  • Under Short Nm: replace 'x' with 'L'.
  • Under Units: type 'cm'

Click on the Options menu and choose zero decimal places from the drop-down menu for Displayed Precision. Choose Done.

Double-click on the letter Y underneath the words "Data Set" in the left-hand side of the screen. Click on the Column Definition table.

  • On the Definitions menu under Name: replace 'Y' with 'Period'.
  • Under Short Nm: replace 'y' with 'T'.
  • Under Units: type 'sec'.

Click on the Options tab.

  • Click on the Point Symbols: Style: drop-down list. Change the open circle to a filled symbol. You will need to scroll up to see these options.
  • From the Color: drop-down list, choose a dark color.
  • From the Displayed Precision: drop-down menu, choose two decimal places.
  • Choose Done.

Click anywhere on the graph to make the gray background become white again.

Step 2: Improving the Look of the Graph

Right-click on the graph and choose Graph Options.

  • Give the graph the title: Period of a Pendulum
  • Click on the Axes Options tab.
  • From the Y-Axis: Scaling drop-down menu, choose 'Autoscale from 0'
  • From the X-Axis: Scaling drop-down menu, choose 'Autoscale from 0'
  • Choose Done.

Step 3: Setting the Axes Manually

The data point for 100 cm should be on the edge of the graph and cut in half. To make all the data points clearly seen in the graphing space, it is possible to set the axes manually.

  • Right-click on the graph and choose Graph Options menu again.
  • Under X-Axis: Scaling drop-down menu, choose 'manual'.
  • Enter '110' for the top number.
  • Choose Done.

If the numerical value of the period for the 100-cm length also causes it to be on the edge of the y-axis (for example, 2.00 sec), then the same set of steps can be used to place the point more clearly within the graphing area.

Step 4: Creating a New Data Column by Calculation

From the Data tab at the top of the page, choose New Calculated Column...

  • Replace 'Calculated Column' with 'Period2'. Use the superscript option from the drop-down menu to add the '2'.
  • Replace 'CC' with 'T2' as the short name and 'sec2' as the unit. Use the superscript option from the drop-down menu to add the '2's.
  • For the Expression: choose 'Period' from the Variables-Columns> drop-down list, enter a star (*) to multiply, then choose 'Period' again from the drop-down list. The sequence "^2" can also be used to square the period.
  • Choose Done.

It is possible to adjust the sizes of the data table and the graph (using the open squares on the borders of each frame) so that all three data columns can be seen at the same time and also don't overlap the graph.

Step 5: Adding a New Graph

From the Page tab at the top of the page, choose Add Page...

  • Use the radio button to Copy Current Page. Choose OK. You will notice that Page 2 is now in the upper left-hand side of the page. If you choose the drop down arrow, there is now a Page 1 and a Page 2.
  • Right-click on the graph to get the Graph Options menu.
  • On the Graph Options tab, change the title of the graph to 'Length vs. the Square of the Period'
  • On the Axes Options tab, uncheck 'Period' and check 'Period2.
  • Change the top number for the 'manual' Y-axis scaling to '4.00' or another value so that all the data points are clearly on the graph.
  • Choose Done.

Step 6: Plotting a Straight Line Through Linear Data

Highlight all the data on the graph by holding down the left mouse button and dragging across the graph. This can also be done to the data columns.

  • When all the data points are highlighted gray, click on the linear fit button at the top of the screen, which is the third from the right-hand side. (If you hover over any icon, it will give you a description of what it does.)
  • Click anywhere on the graph to get rid of the gray background.
  • Move the Linear fit dialog box to a better location.

NOTE: Under Graph Options: Appearance, it is possible to uncheck Point Symbols and to check Connect Points. We rarely do this when constructing a plot from a data table, but it may be the default for some systems when opening the Logger ProTM program.

Save the file as pendulum.cmbl in an appropriate location.

Conclusions

According to an article on the meter in wikipedia.org, in 1671 the French astronomer Jean Picard proposed the length of a two-second pendulum should be a standard length, which he called the toise. However, due to variations in the measurement at the time, that standard was never adopted. It is probably not a coincidence that the much-less-accurate definition of a meter as one ten-millionth the distance between the north pole and the equator is very close to Picard's 'toise'.

Therefore, within our experimental error, the period of a meter-long simple pendulum should be 2.00 seconds. Was your measured value close to this?

Answer the following two questions on the Report Sheet.

1) An analysis of the simple pendulum shows that the slope of the line on the second plot with L on the x-axis and T2 on the y-axis should be:

slope = 4 π2/g

where g is the acceleration due to gravity near the surface of the earth. Solve this equation for g and determine its value from the slope of the line. Find the percent error from the accepted value of 980 cm/s2 using the equation:

% error = [|correct value - experimental value|/correct value] x 100

For this data set, the slope of the line is 0.0408 s2/cm, so g = 4π2/0.0408 = 967 cm/s2 with a percent error =[|980-967|/980] x 100 = 13%

2) If a one-meter pendulum has a period of two seconds, what should be the length of a pendulum with a length of one second? (Hint: the square of the period is proportional to the length of the pendulum: T2 ∝ L.)
